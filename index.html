Europe’s Smart Cities and Cyber Security

Executive Summary

Overview

This project compares eight countries Germany, Estonia, France, Spain, Poland, Netherlands, Italy and Romania across key cybersecurity indicators. These indicators are: recency of each National Cybersecurity Strategy (NCSS), integration of smart city considerations, institutional capacities (e.g. Information sharing and analysis centers (ISACs) and cyber agencies), the scope of critical sectors covered, emphasis on urban resilience and local government roles, public–private partnerships (PPPs), and international cybersecurity rankings.

Our findings reveal diversity in approaches of selected countries. Different countries have completely different plans for integrating modern technology in their urban and suburban centers of life. Moreover, their plans for cybersecurity integrations differ greatly with some leading in strategy rankings while others lagging behind at local city or national levels.

Key Findings

Germany and Netherlands emerge as leaders in smart city cyber security integrations mainly due to explicitly addressing smart cities/IoT in their strategy briefs and fostering multiple ISACs. [1]

Estonia, despite being a small country, astonishes with its holistic approach to security design and top scores in global index rankings.[2]

France has invested greatly in cybersecurity (€1 billion plan and a cyber campus hub) and introduced pilot programs for the security of local communities. [3]

Spain has focused on infrastructure resilience and increasing cybersecurity awareness for citizens and businesses. [4]

Poland and Italy emphasize building robust national systems and PPPs. Poland even highlights securing “Smart Cities, Industry 4.0” in their strategy.[5] 

Italy’s National Cybersecurity Agency frames cybersecurity as an imperative and a development enabler deeming it “not a cost but an investment”.[6]
Romania, while planning to catch up in 2022 strategy, still lags behind others in international metrics. She is working on basic security capacities and public private cooperation.[7]

Recommendations

The EU and national policymakers should promote further best cybersecurity practices. The models of nations that stand out in excellence like Germany securing municipal IoT infrastructure or Netherlands’ trusted ISAC networks, should be promoted across other member states.

Strategy should explicitly include smart city resilience through a built in approach as cities digitalize more and more.

Local governments should be supported and included more in national cyber frameworks. France offers a great model for local entity projects, worth expanding EU wide.

Some countries show high scores for strategies(Global Cybersecurity Index GCI) but low local uptake (low smart city integration). These gaps between policy and practice should be bridged by investments in municipal level and fostering PPPs with local stakeholders.
A coordinated EU wide approach on cyber resilience and cross border ISAC collaborations can help raise standards for all nations.

Introduction

Cybersecurity is a cornerstone of national security and public welfare and more and more digitalization takes over Europe's economies and societies. This report is a structured comparison of eight EU member states’ cybersecurity measures with a focus on urban smart cities. Each of these countries has put forth at least one National Cybersecurity Strategy (NCSS) in the past decade to match the ever changing challenges. We aim to identify patterns, gaps and best practices by comparing metrics ranging from scope of strategies to international index scores.

The primary objectives of this report are:

  1. Comparison and evaluation of how each country’s NCSS addresses smart city security and critical services
  2. Assessment of institutional capacity for cybersecurity (number of ISACs, national cybersecurity organizations and PPPs)
  3. Examination of local government involvement in cybersecurity.
  4. Benchmarking countries against international indices (ITU’s Global Cybersecurity Index and e-Governance Academy’s National Cyber Security Index, specifically the Essential Services Security component)

Ultimately, the report is for policymakers about approaches yielding best cybersecurity and areas needing more attention. We also provide recommendations for both national and EU wide support measures.

Methodology

We chose eight representative countries from the EU for this analysis. The countries were chosen from different geographical as well as socioeconomic profiles across Europe. Our data collection was limited to online resources freely made available in absence of means to gather data on ground or interview key people.  

Data Collection

A comparative matrix of eight countries was constructed using official documents, governmental websites and reputable analysers. Key data came from the latest National Cybersecurity Strategy NCSS of each country. These reports helped form metrics of comparison through the combing of their content (e.g references to “smart cities”). Further analysis of these strategies gave rise to a metric Smart City Integration Score on a 0-2 scale(0=no mention, 1=limited integration, 2=clear integration with concrete measures). National Information Sharing and Analysis Centers ISACs were identified through the European Union Agency for Cybersecurity ENISA website along with National Cybersecurity Organizations in each country. 15 critical infrastructure sectors were identified that align with EU guidelines and each country was assigned a Critical Sectors Listed score based on how many of them they explicitly plan for in their NCSS reports. Urban Resilience Planning was judged qualitatively (Low, Medium, High) based on strategy content about urban or municipal cybersecurity  preparedness. Local Government Involvement was noted as Yes, Partial or No and a number of Public Private Partnerships PPPs were taken from ENISA resources.

Analysis Approach

The analysis is thematically grouped. We first compare smart city readiness, checking strategy texts for smart city or IoT mentions and related initiatives. Next, we compare institutional capacity, the presence of ISACs, number of agencies or coordinating bodies, and overall governance model for cybersecurity (centralized vs. decentralized). We then examine resilience and local involvement, evaluating any dedicated measures for city level resilience, the extent of local authority engagement, and examples of PPPs (e.g joint industry and government initiatives). Finally, we incorporate international benchmarks by compiling each country’s Global Cybersecurity Index (GCI) score (a measure of national cyber commitment and capacity) and the National Cyber Security Index (NCSI) Essential Services Score (which reflects preparedness in securing essential services) – allowing us to see if national efforts align with global best practices. All data points were cross-verified where possible (for example, strategy mentions were cross-checked against summaries from the EU Agency ENISA and independent analyses).

Limitations

It should be noted that the NCSS documents differ in publication date (ranging from 2015 to 2022), so some countries’ data may not reflect very recent policy shifts. Also, the count of ISACs and organizations is approximate as definitions vary by country (some include military cyber commands or intelligence units, others count only civilian agencies). Despite these nuances, the methodology provides a balanced, multi-faceted comparison, sufficient for identifying broad strengths and weaknesses in each nation’s approach.

Comparative Findings

Smart City Readiness

There was a variation in how national strategies address smart city cybersecurity. In the selected eight countries, Germany explicitly includes smart cities in 2021 NCSS. It not only addresses “smart home and smart city” as emerging application areas but also sets concrete measures to secure municipal IoT infrastructure. German strategy plans for analysis of existing municipal IoT systems and development of security recommendations for “key enabling technologies and platforms” in smart cities. This comprehensive vision for smart city cybersecurity made Germany the leading country in the metric of Smart City Integration Score. Poland was one other country that recognised smart cities in its 2019 strategy as a future tech. It emphasized a need for R&D in securing “the Internet of Things, Smart Cities, Industry 4.0” alongside 5G and cloud computing. However, Poland does not have much integration at the moment earning the score of 1.

Other countries have little to no integration of smart cities in their primary cybersecurity strategy. Estonia managed to earn a 1 for their 2024 strategy which plans on treating their whole nation as a ‘digital city’.  Spain 2019 and Italy’s 2022 NCSS do not focus on smart cities. They choose to focus on other critical national services and digital transformation of public administration. France’s 2021 cyber initiative also did not focus on smart cities.

Fig. Smart City Integration Score by country on scale (0-2)

Integrating smart city considerations is increasingly critical as municipal services (traffic control, smart grids, CCTV networks, public Wi-Fi, etc.) become digitally interconnected. The absence of “smart city” in many strategies does not mean cities are ignored as usually, general critical infrastructure measures still apply. However, explicit inclusion (as by Germany) typically indicates higher readiness to tackle unique urban threats (e.g. attacks on smart traffic systems or city IoT sensors). It also often correlates with assigning responsibility and resources to help local authorities upgrade their cyber posture.

Institutional Capacity

Information Sharing and Analysis Centers (ISACs) are a critical part of institutional capacity for threat intel sharing between companies and governments. The Netherlands stand out in this area with several ISACs, some established as far back as the 2010s. Dutch ISACs cover sectors like energy, finance, water, telecom, transport and others. The National Cyber Security Center (NCSC-NL) facilitates between all groups. Netherlands has above 10 ISACs scoring the highest in our report. Germany has the UP-KRITIS partnership that brings together different sector based information sharing networks. France encourages the formation of public private partnership for critical infrastructure security. Recently, France launched the Campus Cyber in 2022, a physical and collaborative hub hosting over 160 public and private players, this acts as a nationwide sharing and innovation platform, and while not an ISAC for one sector, it greatly facilitates cross sector information exchange. Countries like Spain, Poland, Italy and Estonia have set up some information sharing networks like Spain’s National Cyber security council and a security industry CERT. Romania is in the early stages of ISAC development and does not have any well known formal ISAC.

Fig. (A)Number of Information Sharing and Analysis Centers(ISACs) (B)National Coordination Score(0-3)

All eight countries have established National Cybersecurity Agencies. They differ from ISACs in their specific cyber security focus and disaster management efforts in case of a failure but there can still be an overlap.

France and Italy have created agencies centralized in scope. France’s National Agency for Information Systems Security (ANSSI) has been active since 2009. Italy’s National Cybersecurity Agency (ACN) is pretty new as it was founded in 2021. It was a consolidation of the previous fragmented system. These central agencies act as linchpin for other local cybersecurity efforts. Germany has a Federal office for Information Security (BSI) with growing influence which also coordinates with their military operations in addition to civil security. In addition to that, Germany has also set up other coordination efforts for intelligence sharing. Spain has a dual structure where National Cybersecurity Institute (INCIBE) focuses on citizens and the private sector while Cybersecurity Coordination Center (CCN-CERT) focuses on government and critical sectors. Poland similarly has a multi-agency system defined by its 2018 National Cybersecurity Council. Government coordinator, a Cybersecurity Council and civilian and military CERTs work together. Estonoia has a robust system where the Information System Authority (RIA) commands the Estonian Computer Emergency Readiness Team (CERT) leading national cyber strategy. In addition, the Estonian Ministry of Defence also oversees cyber defences. The Netherlands has the National Cybersecurity Center (NCSC-NL)under the ministry of Justice and Security and a Cyber Security Council (CSR) composed of public private members to advise on strategy. The Netherlands also established a Digital Trust Center for business and an ICT response board for crises. Romania after its 2022 strategy, reorganized by creating a National DIrectorate for Cybersecurity (DNSC). In addition, the Romanian Intelligence Agency (SRI) runs a Cyberintelligence Center for critical systems.

In light of such diversity in means and goals, a separate measure than simply the number of cyber security agencies was needed. Based on each country’s NCSS we calculated a National Coordination Maturity Score. A national lead cyber authority can score a country 1 point, existence of a nationwide cyber coordination council a further 1 point, and a final point can be scored if a cyber crisis management unit exists.

Public Private Partnerships

PPPs are vital for resilience in cybersecurity because the private sector operates much of critical infrastructure and has technical expertise. Romania’s 2022 strategy has an intense focus on “strengthening public–private partnerships in order to ensure an open and secure cyberspace” and it is reflected in their high number of PPPs as per ENISA. Spain and Netherlands similarly focus on partnerships with the private sector. Estonia’s model has PPPs woven into the cybersecurity (many of its digital solutions are built by private-sector contractors, and the country’s cyber defense league includes IT professionals from industry volunteering in crises). Germany and France have long standing PPP structures with direct information flow. Italy and Poland have heavily encouraged PPPs in their strategies.

Resilience and Local Involvement

Urban Resilience Planning metric was developed to measure cyber resilience at the city or regional level and how developed they are per countries’ strategies. Resilience in this context means the ability to withstand and recover from cyber incidents, especially in critical services (power, transport, water, etc.) that often have local dimensions. France explicitly incorporates urban resilience in its cybersecurity strategy 2021. By late 2024, France had launched 3 demonstrator projects in the health sector targeting local hospitals or health agencies, to model and improve their cyber resilience . This proactive outreach to municipalities and regions, backed by funding (EUR 20 million allocated), earned France a “High” rating in Urban Resilience Planning. It demonstrates recognition that cities and local entities need resources and templates to bolster their cyber defenses. Germany while not explicitly mentioning urban resilience nevertheless focuses on it. For example, Germany requires that cities’ existing IoT infrastructure be analyzed and securely expanded , effectively boosting urban resilience by design. Germany also has a federal system where states (Länder) and cities conduct cyber exercises earning a “High”. The approach is less direct for other countries like Spain and Estonia who have plans for urban resilience but aren’t addressed explicitly earning “Medium” scores. Netherlands was rated “Medium High” as it runs cybersecurity resilience programs for municipalities. Cities like Hague work closely with national agencies like NCSC. Poland scored “Low Medium” as it is working on a new cybersecurity operations center and Italy a “Medium” for plans exist for public sector resilience but implementation is just beginning. Romania was marked “Low” as it has begun to build fundamental capabilities but lacks dedicated cyber units.

(B)Local Government Involvement

Local Government Involvement measures whether local authorities(cities, municipalities) have a defined role or inclusion in national cybersecurity governance. France clearly stands out: it recognizes local authorities as stakeholders to be supported and included. The existence of those demonstrator projects with local communities  and the integration of regional representatives in the National Cybersecurity Council (France’s council includes local government representatives) justifies a “Yes” for France. Germany, being federal, involves states (and indirectly local governments) in cybersecurity. The NCSS calls for cooperation with municipal IT providers and includes city infrastructure in standards (as noted). Germany has also run awareness programs for municipalities via the BSI. So we also assign “Yes”. Netherlands is also a “Yes”. Dutch municipalities are often directly engaged; for example, there is a Municipal CSIRT (GEMMA) project and the Association of Netherlands Municipalities (VNG) has a cybersecurity agenda in partnership with the NCSC. The Dutch “Cybersecurity Council” even at times discusses local digital resilience. Estonia’s unitary system means local governments implement national standards (for instance, all Estonian local e-services use the secure X Road platform by national design). While there isn’t a separate city cyber program, local gov is inherently part of the national integrated system earning a “Partial”. Same goes for Spain. After 2019, Spain created a Cybersecurity Forum that includes private sector and academia, and there have been calls to involve regional governments, but direct city-level involvement in national strategy execution is not very explicit. Italy’s ACN is meant to interface with all levels of government, and the strategy speaks of securing the “PA and productive fabric” which includes local administrations . However, Italian municipalities vary greatly in capability; the strategy mandates security measures for local public services (especially with the push for digital public services nationally). It’s a work in progress as ACN has started offering services to local governments (like security audits for municipalities). We considered that a “Partial” involvement at this stage. Poland’s cybersecurity system law mainly assigns roles to national and sector entities; local governments are not prominent in the strategic cybersecurity conversation yet earning a “No”. Romania is also a “No” as there’s scant evidence of direct local gov involvement in the national strategy. Their focus is on central institutions and operators of critical infrastructure.

International Benchmarks

We looked at two major international indices Global Cybersecurity Index (GCI) by International telecommunication Union (ITU) and National Cybersecurity Index (NCSI) published by the e-Governance Academy Foundation (eGA) specifically the Essential Services Security (ESS) score. They provide an external measure of a country’s cybersecurity measures. Using two independent metrics also allows us to help identify any misalignments (for example, a country with a high GCI but low ESS might suggest strong policies but weaker implementation in protecting essential services).

As of the most recent GCI assessment published in 2021, the countries in our study span a wide range of rankings. Estonia (99.48) and Spain (98.52)  stand out with 3rd and 4th rank globally. France (97.6) is ranked 9th globally which means three out of our eight nations are ranked in top 10. Germany (97.41) is ranked 13th , Netherlands (97.05) 16th, Italy (96.13) 20th, Poland (93.86) 30th, and Romania (76.29) trails all with 62nd place. However, even 62nd place is mid tier globally, reflecting well on Europe.

  Fig. GCI and NCSI Scores respectively

The NCSI evaluates very practical aspects of cybersecurity: presence of key capacities like incident response, cybercrime units, and crucially, protection of essential services (like power grids, healthcare, water, etc.). For our analysis, we considered the “Essential Services Security (ESS)” component as indicative of how well countries have implemented measures to secure those 15 critical sectors. NCSI-ESS results differ from GCI due to different evaluation criteria.

Discussion

We analysed the data gathered above across eight countries and several patterns, contrasts and gaps emerge.

Regional Contrasts

Western and Northern European countries (France, Germany, Netherlands, Estonia) generally show more robust and mature cybersecurity systems compared to the rest of Europe (Italy, Spain, Poland, Romania). However, this gap is closing. Historically, Estonia and Netherlands invested early in cybersecurity due to cybersecurity attacks; Estonia’s 2007 cyberattack and Netherlands after the DigiNotar incident in 2011. This gave them a lead in developing comprehensive strategies, which is reflected in consistently high GCI/NCSI scores. Southern and Eastern Europe started a bit later; for instance, Italy’s first dedicated cyber strategy only came in 2013 and was then overhauled in 2022, and Romania’s first strategy was in 2013 with a major update in 2021/22. This staggered timeline means at the time of our matrix, countries like Romania were still building basics while others were innovating on advanced topics (AI and Quantum security). However, EU wide initiatives (like the NIS Directive of 2016 and NIS2 of 2022) have significantly evened this out by forcing minimum standards. Now Spain and Poland focus on nearly as many essential cybersecurity measures as France or Germany. This really  demonstrates EU legislation’s impact.

There is some regional nuance in the approach to cybersecurity. Baltic and Nordic regions (Estonia) tend to emphasize societal resilience(public awareness, education) as contrast to Central Europe(Poland, Europe) which has a focus on critical infrastructure protection and cyber defence. Latin Europe (France, Italy, Spain) traditionally had more state centric approaches but are now opening up to private sectors and citizen participation more. Ransomware attacks on the French and Italian health systems might have influenced these decisions.

High GCI vs Low Urban Integration

One striking pattern we identified is that international rankings don’t always tell the full story when it comes to how cybersecurity is experienced on the ground at local levels. Spain exemplifies “high GCI but low urban integration.” Spain earned a top-five GCI rank ,meaning on paper it has excellent laws, standards, national frameworks, yet our analysis showed that Spain had not explicitly integrated “smart city” or local government involvement into its strategy by 2019. This suggests a gap between national policy and local implementation. Spain’s essential services (power, finance, etc.) are likely well regulated (It has high NCSI as well), but if a Spanish city’s smart traffic system came under attack, there might not have been a clear blueprint from the NCSS on how to handle it.

Crisis Management

From ransomware to state-sponsored espionage, all these countries have faced real cyber incidents in recent years which tested their resilience. A pattern we note is that countries with established national CERTs and clear incident protocols responded more effectively and used incidents to refine their approach.

Recommendations

Based on our comparative analysis, we propose a set of recommendations targeting both the EU and the nations to enhance cybersecurity.

Integration of smart city security into national frameworks

Countries could benefit from explicitly including smart cities and local digital infrastructure into their cybersecurity strategies. The EU, possibly through ENISA,  should create guidelines or a toolkit for urban cybersecurity modeled on Germany's municipal IoT security analysis and funding from EU programs could support city pilot projects. National governments should promote cybersecurity plans for cities, including a National Municipal Cybersecurity Program to connect national cyber agencies with local IT departments and provide support, thereby enhancing overall cybersecurity consistency across different municipal services.

Strengthening of ISAC networks

Information Sharing and Analysis Centers have proven their worth in countries like the Netherlands. Policymakers should mandate or incentivize the creation of ISACs in all key sectors, including some emerging ones (like a potential Healthcare ISAC, called for after many hospital attacks in Europe). The EU can support a European ISAC Network that links national ISACs for cross border sharing (cyber threats don’t stop at borders, as seen with attacks that propagate globally). Nationally, where sector ISACs don’t exist, governments can play matchmaker, e.g, convene companies in the transport sector to form an ISAC with the national CERT as a cohost. Provide financial support or tax incentives for participation in ISACs (since it requires companies to devote staff time). Also, emphasize ISACs for local government or regional alliances This recommendation builds on observed success: Netherlands’ multi ISAC model and Poland's practice of involving the private sector via trust-building.

Enhancement of PPPs

While all countries currently have PPPs, their effectiveness varies. Policymakers should establish formal PPP frameworks with clear objectives. For instance, create a National Cybersecurity Advisory Board that includes CEOs of critical infrastructure companies, big tech providers, and defense contractors, alongside government officials and academics. Quite like the Netherlands’ Cyber Security Council or France’s industry inclusive strategy planning . These boards can advise on strategy updates, but more tangibly, joint task forces can be formed for specific challenges (e.g a task force on securing 5G networks that includes telecom operators, or on cloud security including cloud providers). The EU could facilitate cross country PPP exchanges like an EU wide workshop series where PPP initiatives from different countries share lessons (Germany’s UP KRITIS, France’s Campus Cyber etc can serve as case studies). An important part of PPP is information sharing from government to industry. We recommend that national agencies develop protocols to declassify and share threat intelligence with trusted industry partners more swiftly. Several strategies have already noted it as a goal so implementation should be the next step.

Bolstering local government cyber capacities

EU and national authorities should invest in capacity building programs for local governments. This includes training IT staff in municipalities on cybersecurity, funding dedicated cybersecurity officer positions in city administrations, and developing guidelines for local incident response. The EU could create a Municipal Cybersecurity Fund to finance various projects like the deployment of security monitoring tools in smaller cities which cannot afford them. On the national level, consider initiatives like Cyber Resilience Grants for cities like how some countries fund local climate resilience. These grants could help a town upgrade its firewall or conduct a cybersecurity assessment. Another recommendation is establishing a Cyber Reserve at regional level. Skilled volunteers or units (similar to Estonia’s model) can assist local authorities during a major cyber incident. We also suggest formalizing local involvement in strategy governance like inclusion of a representative of the national association of municipalities in the National Cybersecurity Council). This ensures local needs are heard when national policies are crafted. The patterns we saw (France including local projects, Germany involving states) should be generalized where every country should make local governments an active stakeholder in cybersecurity planning.

Improvements in Incident reporting and response mechanisms

Governments should refine channels like 24/7 hotlines at national CERTs for critical operators. Consider adopting a page from aviation safety: a cyber incident anonymous reporting database where organizations can share near misses or vulnerabilities found, analyzed by a neutral body (perhaps ENISA at EU level) to produce lessons learned for all. On response, each country should regularly update and rehearse its national cyber incident response plan. Cross border response is also crucial. EU’s CyCLONe (Cyber Crisis Liaison Organization Network) is a step in coordinating major incident responses among nations. All EU countries should fully engage with it. The pattern of joint exercises (e.g Locked Shields which teams from these nations participate in) should be expanded to include critical infrastructure companies and even municipal IT teams in the drills. By doing so, gaps in crisis communications or technical mitigations can be identified and fixed proactively.

Full Implementation of NIS2

EU policymakers (and national transposers) should ensure that the NIS2 Directive is thoroughly implemented by 2024/2025 in all member states, extending security requirements to the new sectors (like municipalities, space, food, etc.) and smaller entities down the supply chain. This will directly address the “critical sectors coverage” gap for any country that was selective before.

Focusing on emerging threats

Policymakers must future-proof strategies. We recommend each country establish a horizon scanning unit that specifically looks at emerging tech and threats like AI misuse, post quantum crypto risks, IoT vulnerabilities, etc. The EU can coordinate this via ENISA’s threat landscape reports and a joint research agenda. For AI, specifically, integrate cybersecurity into the EU’s AI Act guidelines. Encourage countries to experiment with AI for cybersecurity (like using machine learning for anomaly detection in networks) through funded pilot projects, so they are not left behind by adversaries who are certainly weaponizing AI.

Another emerging area is cyber aspects of hybrid warfare (disinformation combined with cyber attacks). Some strategies like that of France’s already cover it. Others should incorporate it too.  Countries with space assets (France, Germany, Italy) should develop dedicated plans for space cybersecurity and share knowledge with others.

Strengthening EU and NATO coordination

Given all these countries are in the EU and most in NATO, leverage those alliances more. For EU, support the new Joint Cyber Unit initiative to improve collective response to large scale attacks. For NATO, continue joint exercises and intelligence sharing through CCDCOE which these nations contribute to. We recommend creating national dissemination channels for EU cybersecurity developments. For example, after each EU Cybersecurity Cooperation Group meeting, a summary is sent to all relevant national stakeholders (agencies, sector regulators, etc.) so that everyone stays aligned with best practices and directives.

Monitor and evaluate progress

Finally, we recommend instituting an annual cybersecurity readiness review for each country. his would track metrics like those in our matrix (strategy updated or not, new ISACs formed, number of cyber incidents at local level, GCI/NCSI trends, etc.). By publishing these scorecards (or even a EU-wide ranking beyond GCI/NCSI), policymakers and the public can gauge improvement and identify lingering weak spots. This can help identify growth as well as any place a country is lagging behind. Policymakers should therefore treat this as a collective mission, embodying the mantra that Europe is only as secure as its least secure link and working to strengthen that weakest link.


Conclusion

This report analyzes the cybersecurity measures taken by Germany, Estonia, France, Spain, Poland, the Netherlands, Italy, and Romania. We found a generally positive trajectory: all countries recognize cybersecurity as a national priority and have taken concrete steps in recent years to bolster their defenses. European cooperation and standards by EU bodies have a positive impact resulting in high Global Cybersecurity Index rankings of most and the widespread adoption of critical infrastructure protections . At the same time, the analysis exposed clear differentiators: countries that adopted early and holistic strategies (Estonia, France, Germany) tend to perform best, especially when they integrate stakeholders at all levels and anticipate emerging issues. Delays and approaching cybersecurity piecemeal means some countries like Romania are still catching up.

Key themes include the necessity of smart city security, public-private partnerships, and continuous capacity building. The matrix indicators, visuals, and case studies consistently pointed to the fact that cybersecurity is not just a technical challenge but a governance challenge that spans from central governments down to city halls, and from large telecom companies down to individual citizens.

Smart cities represent a new frontier that needs immediate attention. This report illustrates how an absence of explicit planning for municipal systems can be a blind spot even for otherwise secure nations. Institutional capacity in terms of agencies and ISACs is generally strong, but what separates good from great is collaboration between private companies and flow of information across critical sectors.

The most encouraging insight, in our analysis, is that each country had at least one exemplary practice that others can learn from: Germany’s strategic foresight on industrial and smart tech security , Estonia’s culture of cyber hygiene , France’s ambitious investments and local outreach , Spain’s comprehensive strategy objectives tying cybersecurity to citizens’ safety , Poland’s legal and organizational overhaul aligning with EU directives , Netherlands’ multi-layered cooperation, Italy’s integration of security with economic development goals , and Romania’s recent strides leveraging EU support to reform its cyber framework. These practices can be encouraged across the EU and the recommendations can be  codified by central organizers like ENISA.
